> **Not policies. Principles.** Policies say what to do and can be amended
> by process; principles are the **physics of the Enterprise** — the
> forces every design must work within. A policy that violates a
> principle is misdrafted; a structure that violates one is misdesigned;
> a decision that violates one is wrong even if it succeeds. Every future
> decision, document, entity, and automation can be tested against this
> page.

---

## The eleven principles

### 1. Simplicity over complexity

Complexity is where risk, cost, and dishonesty hide. The simplest
structure that achieves the required protection wins; every added entity,
layer, tool, or exception must pay for its complexity with named value.
**Test:** can the Founder explain this design to a family member in two
minutes? **Violated when:** structures exist that no one can state the
purpose of; a third tool does what one did.

### 2. Separate ownership from operations

Assets consolidate toward protected holders; operating risk stays
quarantined inside operating companies (PEGS-150.001 §1; PEGS-152 §1.3).
The building must survive the tenant's worst day.
**Test:** if this venture failed completely, what else would it pull
down? (Right answer: nothing.) **Violated when:** century assets sit
inside operating risk envelopes; one entity's crisis can reach another's
assets.

### 3. Never mix charitable and commercial activities

The Foundation owns mission, never operations; commerce never launders
itself through charity, and charity never subsidizes commerce
(PEGS-152 §2.5). One bright line, no exceptions.
**Test:** does any dollar, asset, or benefit cross between charitable
and commercial purposes outside the one-way philanthropy flow
(150.005)? **Violated when:** grants touch insiders; the Foundation
holds anything operational; marketing borrows the charity's halo for a
sale.

### 4. Protect patient trust above enterprise value

Patient trust — data, safety, honesty — outranks every commercial
number, including the Enterprise's own valuation (PEGS-000 Art. III §2;
never-sell list item 4; asset D-01 held as custody, not property).
**Test:** would we accept this if the patient were reading it over our
shoulder? **Violated when:** PHI approaches any commercial use; safety
competes with schedule; a claim exceeds evidence.

### 5. Preserve founder optionality

Doors stay open until closing one buys something the Five Gates can
name. Reversible instruments before irreversible ones; short commitments
before long ones; no lock-in — vendor, capital, or structural — adopted
by default (PEGS-000 Art. V's one-way-door doctrine applied to design).
**Test:** what future choice does this design remove, and what did we
get paid for it? **Violated when:** exclusivity, perpetuity, or
irrevocability appears in any instrument without a Class 2 memo naming
its price.

### 6. Build for succession

Every role has a deputy, every system a document, every asset a target
holder, every plan a version that works without its author. The final
exam is a succession nobody dreads (PEGS-150.009 Level 5; PEGS-101).
**Test:** if the accountable person vanished today, what breaks and for
how long? **Violated when:** anything critical lives in one head; a role
charter has an empty deputy line.

### 7. One source of truth

Every fact has exactly one authoritative location; everything else is a
projection that syncs from it and never originates (PEGS-000 Art. VI §1;
Integration Standard). Two authoritative copies is zero.
**Test:** where is the ONE place this is true, and do all projections
point at it? **Violated when:** a spreadsheet, chat thread, or mirror
quietly becomes where decisions get made.

### 8. Documentation before delegation

Authority transfers only in writing: charter first, then handoff
(L02; PEGS-000 Art. VI §3). Undocumented delegation is abdication —
the work moves but the accountability evaporates.
**Test:** can the delegate point to the written source of their
authority and its limits? **Violated when:** "just handle it" replaces a
charter; a person exercises authority no document grants.

### 9. Automation supports people; it doesn't replace accountability

Every automated system has a named human owner, an inspection method,
and a hard stop at the firewalls (PEGS-000 Art. VI §5; Automation
Catalog rule 1). Machines execute; humans answer.
**Test:** who is accountable for this automation's last output, and when
did they last inspect one? **Violated when:** "the system did it"
appears as an explanation; an automation runs that no one reviews.

### 10. Scale systems before scaling headcount

Heroics → SOP → automation → *then* hiring (PEGS-000 Art. III §5).
Headcount added to a broken process scales the breakage and hides it
longer.
**Test:** is the process this hire will run documented and stable — or
are we hiring someone to absorb chaos? **Violated when:** the org chart
grows faster than the SOP index.

### 11. Stewardship over short-term optimization

Every optimization is tested against the decade, not the quarter
(PEGS-000 Art. III §7; the Legacy Gate). We accept slower, compounding,
inheritable results over faster, extractive, dead-end ones — in
structures, in brands, in people.
**Test:** does this make the Enterprise the next steward inherits
stronger or merely this quarter's numbers better? **Violated when:**
trust, craft, or people are spent to hit a date; a century asset is
risked for a decade gain.

## The Ownership Doctrine (canonical statement)

> **The intended long-term ownership doctrine of the Pascual Enterprise
> is that controlling interests shall ultimately reside within the
> approved enterprise ownership structure, as determined by the Founder
> and implemented with qualified legal and tax counsel.**

This sentence — the Founder's words — is the authoritative expression of
the doctrine. PEGS-152 details its per-class application; PEGS-150.002
maps its current intended implementation (the Trust → Holdings chain);
counsel determines the instruments. Principles 2, 5, 6, and 11 all serve
it.

## How the principles are applied

1. **Class 2 decision memos** add one line per engaged principle
   (most decisions engage 2–4; a memo engaging none is describing a
   Class 3 decision).
2. **Architecture and structure proposals** (new entities, tools,
   automations) test against all eleven before drafting begins.
3. **Conflicts between principles** (e.g., simplicity vs. separation)
   are resolved by PEGS-000's precedence — dignity and integrity first —
   and recorded, so the resolution teaches.
4. Principles are amended only by the PEGS-000 Art. VIII §3 process:
   physics changes rarely, deliberately, and on the record.

## Governance notes

These principles bind design, not just conduct: a compliant document
implementing a principle-violating structure is still wrong. The
alignment checklist (L01) gains a twelfth line at next revision: "Which
design principles does this engage, and does it pass their tests?"
